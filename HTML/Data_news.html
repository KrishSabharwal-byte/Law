The Draft Digital Personal Data Protection Rules, 2025 – Operationalising India’s Data Protection Law

Overview & Regulatory Context

  • On January 3, 2025, the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) released the Draft DPDP Rules to implement the DPDP Act, inviting public feedback until February 18, 2025.
  • The purpose is to provide actionable guidance and clarity around key compliance obligations—such as simplified language, contextual definitions, and illustrative examples (the “SARAL framework”).

Key Provisions

1. Data Protection Obligations & Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs)

  • Data fiduciaries must implement “reasonable safeguards”— including encryption, access controls, audits.
  • SDFs (entities handling large volumes of sensitive personal data) face enhanced obligations like mandatory data audits and risk assessments.

2. Cross-Border Data Transfers

  • Allowed only to government-approved countries, and the government can restrict or prohibit transfers seen as against national interest—reinforcing data sovereignty concerns.

3. Data Retention & Minimisation

  • Data fiduciaries must limit retention to the duration necessary for the original purpose and apply data minimisation principles.

4. Parental Consent for Minors

  • Processing personal data of individuals under 18 requires "verifiable" parental or guardian consent, with technical and organizational due diligence mandated.

5. Enforcement & Penalties

  • Non-compliance may attract fines ranging from ₹50 crore to ₹250 crore depending on the violation—significant for high-stakes corporate compliance.

6. Government Exemptions & RTI Changes

  • The government enjoys broad exemptions; authorities can access personal data without notifying the affected individual.
  • Amendments to the RTI Act remove the “larger public interest” exception for personal data.

7. Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)

  • The Rule framework activates the DPBI, which adjudicates breaches, hears complaints, imposes penalties, and can direct remedial measures or refer disputes to ADR.

Industry Reactions & Implications

  • Trade Associations have flagged concerns over the sudden return of data localisation requirements, arguing it contradicts the more trade-friendly DPDP Act and could hinder global data flow.
  • Financial services players such as Google Pay, PhonePe, Amazon Pay, and NPCI are seeking exemptions from a transaction-level consent requirement, citing operational burdens.
  • Media commentary highlights lingering ambiguity around minors' consent protocols and data export rules.
